What does chmod 600 do? :
What does "600" actually mean?
In Linux, permissions are represented by three digits. Each digit represents a different
"class" of user:
    First Digit (6): The Owner (You).
    Second Digit (0): The Group (Users in your workgroup).
    Third Digit (0): Others (Everyone else on the system).
The numbers themselves are a sum of three possible actions:
    4 = Read
    2 = Write
    1 = Execute
Breaking down the "600":
    6 (4+2): You have permission to Read and Write the file.
    0: The Group has no permissions at all.
    0: Others have no permissions at all.
By running chmod 600 my_key, you are telling the Linux kernel: 
"This file is for my eyes only. Lock everyone else out." 
Once the permissions are set this way, the ssh command is satisfied that the key is secure
and will allow the connection to proceed.
